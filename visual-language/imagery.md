# Cena Health — Imagery System

_Design specification for the role of photography and non-illustrative imagery in the Cena Health brand. Addresses brand brief Q3 directly._

---

## 1. The Decision

### Cena Health uses photography. Selectively, in defined contexts, under strict art direction. It is not the primary visual language — illustration is. But photography is necessary.

**The rationale:**

The brand brief requires two things that illustration alone cannot fully deliver:

1. **Clinical credibility at the institutional register.** Hospital CFOs, health plan executives, and clinical integration leads evaluate Cena Health against other healthcare vendors. Those vendors use photography. A brand that presents only illustration in an investor deck or clinical documentation risks reading as a consumer product, not clinical infrastructure. The illustration system's strength — its warmth, its organic humanity — becomes a vulnerability in rooms where photographic evidence of real partnerships, real facilities, and real operations signals "we exist and we are serious."

2. **Authenticity of specific cultural representation.** The brand demands cultural specificity — not categories but particulars: mole, platanos, greens with cornbread, dumplings (brand brief §1, trait 3). Illustration can depict these with warmth and specificity, but photography can document them with authority. When the brand says "we serve culturally relevant meals," a photograph of a real meal served by a real Cena Health care plan carries evidentiary weight that illustration cannot match.

**What photography is not in this system:**

Photography is not the brand's emotional voice. It does not tell stories, set moods, or create the brand's felt experience. That is illustration's role. Photography is the brand's evidential voice — it proves, documents, and verifies. The emotional register of every Cena Health surface is set by illustration, typography, and color. Photography enters that register as a guest — it conforms to the environment, it does not set the temperature.

## 2. Photography Style

### Art Direction

| Property | Specification |
|----------|--------------|
| **Lighting** | Natural light, diffused. No studio flash, no hard shadows, no rim lighting. Overhead or window-light direction. The light source should feel like a room with good windows, not a photography studio. |
| **Color temperature** | Warm. Daylight white balance shifted slightly warm (approximately +5–10 on warmth sliders, never cool or blue). The photography must integrate with the brand's warm surface system — cool-lit photos on a warm off-white page create tonal dissonance. |
| **Saturation** | Reduced by approximately 15–20% from capture default. Colors remain identifiable but do not pop. This desaturation brings photographic color into the same perceptual range as the illustration palette's muted token values. A photograph should not feel more vivid than an adjacent illustration. |
| **Contrast** | Medium-low. No crushed blacks, no blown highlights. The tonal range should be gentle — darks lifted slightly, highlights softened slightly. The feeling is "printed on warm paper," not "projected on a screen." |
| **Depth of field** | Selective focus with soft background. The subject is sharp; the environment is present but not detailed. This matches the illustration system's depth logic (foreground detail, background simplification) and prevents photographs from being more visually complex than adjacent illustrations. |
| **Composition** | Follows the illustration system's composition patterns. Off-center subjects for people and food. Left-to-right flow for process documentation. Generous negative space (minimum 25% of frame, compatible with the spacing system's generous-by-default principle). |

### Color Treatment

Photographs are post-processed to integrate with the brand palette. This is not aggressive color grading — it is a calibration that ensures photographic color does not fight the teal-sage-warm token system.

**Treatment specification:**

1. **Highlights shift toward warm neutral 900** (`#F3F1EE`). The brightest areas of a photograph should approach the cream of the brand's surface system, not pure white. This is achieved through highlight tone adjustment, not by overlaying a color tint.
2. **Shadows shift toward teal-200** (`#0D322D`). The darkest areas carry a faint green cast — the same chromatic dark that governs the brand's text system. This prevents shadows from reading as neutral gray or cool blue, either of which would introduce color foreign to the palette.
3. **Midtones remain naturalistic** with a slight warm bias. Skin tones, food colors, and environmental colors must read as true — the treatment does not impose a visible color cast on the subject. The shifts in highlights and shadows are the frame; the midtones are the content.
4. **Green vegetation, if present, should skew toward sage** (H:145–148) rather than pure green (H:130). This prevents outdoor or plant-adjacent photography from introducing a green that competes with the brand's sage family.

### What Photography Cannot Show

| Reject | Reason |
|--------|--------|
| Posed, studio-lit portraits | Contradicts the natural, documentary tone. People in photographs should appear mid-action, not mid-pose. |
| Stock photography "diversity staging" | The brand brief explicitly rejects "adding 'diverse' stock photos as a surface treatment" (brand brief §2). No multi-ethnic group shots where everyone is smiling at the camera. |
| Before/after compositions | The illustration system rejects these (seed document, Emotional Architecture). Photography follows the same rule. No weight loss photos, no "sad then happy" pairings. |
| Clinical equipment as the subject | No stethoscope close-ups, no hospital bed shots, no equipment glamor. Per the seed document's healthcare guidance. |
| Overhead flat-lay food styling | This is Instagram food photography's signature composition. It reads as consumer food brand, not healthcare infrastructure. Food is photographed in context — on tables, in hands, in kitchens. |
| Pure white or cool gray backgrounds | All photography sits in warm-toned environments. No product-on-white-background shots. |
| Unattributable stock photography | Every photograph in the system should be either (a) original, shot under the art direction specified here, or (b) sourced with a clear license and treated to meet these specifications. Generic healthcare stock that could belong to any health brand does not belong to this one. |

## 3. Photography Contexts

Photography is permitted in specific contexts. Outside these contexts, illustration is the default.

### 3.1 Partner and Team Documentation

**What:** Photographs of real Cena Health team members, real partner health system facilities (with permission), real community health center environments, and real partnership moments (signing events, pilot launches, team meetings).

**Why:** These prove the brand's claims. "We partner with UConn Health and Vanderbilt Health" is stronger when accompanied by a real photograph from that partnership than by an illustration of a generic hospital.

**Treatment:** Full art direction specification applies. Partner logos visible in environment shots are acceptable — they are evidence, not decoration.

### 3.2 Food Documentation

**What:** Photographs of culturally specific meals prepared as part of Cena Health care plans. The actual food, in the actual vessels, prepared in the actual style.

**Why:** When the brand claims cultural competence in nutrition, these photographs are the proof. A photograph of the mole, the actual greens, the specific dumplings — these images do work that illustration supports but cannot replace. The viewer sees "they actually make this food" — not "they drew a picture of this food."

**Treatment:** Natural light. On a table or counter, not on a photography backdrop. Hands may be visible (preparing, serving, reaching). Minimal styling — the food should look home-cooked, not restaurant-plated. The seed document's food guidance applies: "home cooking environments, not restaurant presentation."

### 3.3 Investor and Institutional Materials

**What:** Selective photography in investor decks, partnership proposals, and clinical documentation where photographic evidence strengthens the credibility argument.

**Why:** The institutional audience expects photographic proof of operations. An investor deck with only illustration may read as "early stage concept" rather than "operating infrastructure." A single well-placed photograph of a real care delivery moment, a real team, or a real partner facility anchors the deck in reality.

**Treatment:** These photographs are used sparingly — typically 2–4 per deck, not on every slide. They appear in specific moments: the "our team" slide, the "our partners" slide, the "in action" slide. The rest of the deck uses illustration and data.

### 3.4 Contexts Where Photography Is Not Used

| Context | Visual language instead |
|---------|----------------------|
| Website hero sections | Illustration (narrative scenes) |
| Patient-facing materials | Illustration exclusively. No photography in care plans, meal guides, enrollment flows, or VozCare-related surfaces. |
| App UI | Illustration (spot illustrations, icons) |
| Slide deck backgrounds | Illustration (background textures) or solid surface colors |
| Social media (organic content) | Illustration with occasional food photography |
| Empty states and onboarding | Illustration |
| Clinical integration documentation | Illustration (diagram illustrations) with optional photography only for partner-specific context |

**The governing principle:** Photography appears where the brand needs to prove something real. Illustration appears where the brand needs to express something felt. Most of the brand's surface area is expression, not proof — so most of the brand is illustrated.

## 4. Photography and Illustration Together

Photographs and illustrations may appear on the same page, the same slide, or the same screen — but they never overlap, blend, or merge.

### Coexistence Rules

1. **Photographs and illustrations occupy separate spatial zones.** A photograph sits in its own container (card, image well, bordered region). An illustration sits in its own container or bleeds into the page surface. They do not overlap, composite, or share a container.

2. **No photographic textures in illustrations.** The illustration system rejects this explicitly (illustration.md §7). No photo-illustration hybrids.

3. **No illustrated overlays on photographs.** No drawn frames around photos, no illustrated borders, no illustrated elements extending from a photograph's edge. The photograph is treated as a documentary artifact; the illustration is treated as a brand expression. They are different registers of the same voice, not a collage.

4. **A page with both should lead with illustration.** If a website section or slide shows both a narrative illustration and a food photograph, the illustration sits higher in the visual hierarchy (larger, more prominently placed). The photograph is supporting evidence. The illustration is the emotional lead. This follows the brand's hierarchy: expression first, evidence second.

5. **Color treatment bridges the gap.** The photography color treatment (§2) is calibrated so that photographs and illustrations share the same warm-tone-biased color world. When a photograph of a meal sits on the same page as an illustration of a family preparing food, they should feel like they belong to the same environment — warm light, muted saturation, teal-shifted darks. This color kinship is what allows the two visual languages to coexist without collision.

### What Photography Handles That Illustration Would Struggle With

- **Proof of specific partnerships and operations.** Illustration can depict "a community health center" but cannot prove "this community health center."
- **Credentialing for institutional audiences.** A real team, a real facility, a real meal — these build the confidence that the brand is operating, not conceptualizing.
- **Food texture and materiality.** Illustration renders food beautifully, but the tactile quality of a real bowl of greens, the specific glisten of a mole, the particular fold of a dumpling — photography captures a sensory quality that drawing approaches from a different angle.

### What Illustration Handles That Photography Would Struggle With

- **Depicting infrastructure invisibly.** The care coordination workflow, the connection between provider and patient and platform — these are visible in illustration's compositional staging and invisible in photography unless staged beyond believability.
- **Agency and emotional nuance.** The seed document's "dignified agency" is easier to guarantee in illustration, where every element is deliberately placed, than in photography, where candid moments may not always communicate the intended emotional register.
- **Cultural specificity without tokenization.** Illustration draws specific people in specific situations. Photography risks the "diverse stock photo" problem the brand brief explicitly rejects — unless every photo is custom-shot, which is operationally expensive.
- **Tonal consistency across surfaces.** Every illustration comes from the same visual system. Photographs, even under strict art direction, carry inherent variability. Illustration provides the brand's consistent surface; photography provides occasional, controlled variability.

## 5. Practical Guidance

### Sourcing

| Source type | Acceptable? | Conditions |
|-------------|------------|------------|
| Custom photography | Preferred | Shot under the art direction specified in §2. Photographer briefed on brand principles. |
| Licensed editorial photography | Acceptable with caution | Must be treatable to match brand color specification. Must not be recognizable as stock. Must depict real environments and real people in non-posed contexts. |
| Generic stock photography | Not acceptable | "Healthcare worker smiling at camera" and its variants are the visual equivalent of "revolutionizing healthcare" — the brand brief rejects both. |
| User-generated photography | Acceptable for social | Community-submitted food photos, with permission, can appear in social contexts. Must be treated per §2 color specification. |
| AI-generated photography | Not acceptable | The brand's commitment to specificity and authenticity is incompatible with generated images. If the brand shows a meal, it should be a meal that was actually prepared. If it shows a team, it should be the actual team. |

### Format Requirements

| Context | Format | Aspect ratio | Min resolution |
|---------|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Website (hero/feature image) | WebP, AVIF fallback to JPEG | 16:9 or 3:2 | 1600px wide at 1x |
| Website (inline/card image) | WebP, AVIF fallback to JPEG | 4:3 or 1:1 | 800px wide at 1x |
| Slide deck | PNG or high-quality JPEG | Match slide aspect (16:9 or 4:3) | 1920px wide |
| Print (brochure, flyer) | TIFF or high-quality JPEG | As needed | 300 DPI at print size |
| Social media | Platform-specific requirements | Platform-specific | Platform minimum |

### Treatment Workflow

For any photograph entering the brand system:

1. **Evaluate fit.** Does this photograph depict something that earns its place per §3 (proof, documentation, food specificity)? If not, commission illustration instead.
2. **Color treat.** Apply the highlight-to-warm-neutral, shadow-to-chromatic-dark, desaturation adjustments specified in §2.
3. **Crop and compose.** Frame per the composition rules: off-center subjects, generous negative space, left-to-right flow.
4. **Context check.** Place the treated photograph adjacent to brand illustration and on the brand's surface system. Does it feel native? If the photograph pulls the eye away from the illustration or feels tonally alien against `surface-page`, retreat the treatment until harmony is achieved.
5. **Approve.** A treated photograph is a brand artifact. It should be reviewed with the same rigor as an illustration commission.
