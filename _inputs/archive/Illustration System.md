## Visual DNA

Four non-negotiable principles that define every illustration:

1. **Warm Clarity** - Simple, legible forms that communicate immediately without visual noise. Every element serves understanding.
2. **Dignified Humanity** - People are portrayed with respect and agency, never as victims or stereotypes. Bodies, abilities, and cultures are authentically diverse.
3. **Grounded Optimism** - Honest about health challenges while conveying achievable progress. Avoids both clinical sterility and unrealistic cheerfulness.
4. **Thematic Cohesion** - Every element within an illustration must feel as though the same illustrator created it. No text overlays, standalone icons, or graphic elements that break the illustrated aesthetic. Simplified elements are acceptable only when they serve the narrative and match the organic, hand-drawn quality of surrounding artwork.

## Emotional Architecture

### Primary: Gentle Empowerment

Patients feel capable and supported, not overwhelmed. Visual language says “you can do this with our help.”

**Achieve through:**
- Forward-leaning body language, not passive or defeated poses
- People actively engaged (preparing food, using technology, connecting with others)
- Composition that places patients as central actors, not recipients
- Scale relationships that make technology/systems feel accessible, not imposing
**Avoid:**
- Heroic/triumphant poses (unrealistic)
- Passive receiving of care (disempowering)
- Overwhelmed or confused expressions
- Visual complexity that creates anxiety

### Secondary: Warm Trust

The feeling of a knowledgeable coach walking alongside, culturally competent and understanding.

**Achieve through:**
- Soft, organic line quality (hand-drawn feel, not geometric rigidity)
- Approachable color temperature (warm neutrals, nature-inspired tones)
- Human-scaled environments (homes, community spaces vs. institutional settings)
- Visual connections between people (eye contact, shared activities, proximity)
**Avoid:**
- Corporate/institutional imagery
- Cold, clinical color palettes
- Isolated individuals without context
- Authoritative/hierarchical spatial relationships

### Tertiary: Dignified Hope

Acknowledging challenges while conveying achievable progress without pity or false cheer.

**Achieve through:**
- Realistic scenarios with positive momentum (mid-journey, not just endpoints)
- Honest portrayal of effort alongside support systems
- Color palette that balances warmth with credibility
- Composition showing progress/direction without completion pressure
**Avoid:**
- Before/after transformation narratives
- Pitying or condescending visual metaphors
- Unrealistic perfection (pristine kitchens, flawless meals)
- Generic “wellness” aesthetics (yoga poses, green smoothies, meditation)

Use lighter/darker shades for depth, shadows, highlights, and tonal variation:

- **Stone range** - Neutral backgrounds, subtle textures, environmental elements

### Color Rules

**Simple Illustrations (Icons/Spots)**
- Maximum 3 colors per illustration
- One dominant color (60%), one supporting (30%), one accent (10%)
- Default: Sage Green dominant + Cream background + Terracotta or Navy accent
- High contrast between foreground and background for accessibility
**Complex Narrative Scenes**
- Maximum 5-6 colors per composition
- Establish clear color hierarchy (background/midground/foreground)
- Use extended palette shades for depth and dimension
- Warm tones (terracotta, orange, coral) for human/food elements
- Cool tones (teal, blue) for technology/systems
- Stone range for environmental grounding
**Cultural Food Representation**
- Use full coral/orange/terracotta range for diverse cuisines
- Avoid relying solely on “healthy green” food stereotypes
- Represent cultural dishes with authentic color accuracy
- Use warm palette to make all foods feel welcoming and appealing
**Accessibility Requirements**
- All text/icon elements must meet WCAG AA contrast ratios (4.5:1 minimum)
- Test against Stone-300 cream background as baseline
- Use Navy-750 or deeper shades for critical information
- Avoid color-only communication (use shape/pattern/texture as well)

## Line & Form Language

### Line Quality

**Weight:** Variable line weight for warmth and hierarchy
- Primary elements: 3-4px stroke
- Secondary details: 2px stroke
- Fine details/textures: 1px stroke
- Never uniform weight across entire illustration
**Style:** Organic with subtle imperfection
- Hand-drawn quality, not geometric precision
- Gentle curves over hard angles
- Slightly irregular edges (not perfectly smooth vectors)
- Confident, flowing strokes vs. tentative sketchy marks
**Caps & Joins:** Rounded
- Round line caps for softness
- Round joins at intersections
- Avoid sharp miters or hard corners

### Shape Philosophy

**Simple Illustrations:**
- Simplified geometric foundations (circles, rounded rectangles, organic blobs)
- 2-4 shapes maximum per icon
- Recognize at thumbnail size (32px)
- Avoid fine detail that disappears when scaled down
**Complex Scenes:**
- Build from simple shapes, add selective detail
- Foreground elements more detailed, background simplified
- Use negative space intentionally for breathing room
- Maximum detail density: 60% of composition (40% calm space)

### Element Integration

**Core Rule:** No text or icons within illustrations. All visual elements must share the same illustrative DNA.

**Prohibited Elements:**
- Text labels, captions, or typography overlaid on or embedded in illustrations
- Standalone icons or UI-style graphics mixed with illustrated content
- Geometric symbols that feel designed rather than drawn
- Infographic elements (arrows, badges, callout boxes) within narrative scenes
- Any element that looks like it belongs to a different visual system

**Acceptable Simplification:**
- Simplified objects that serve the narrative (e.g., a stylized phone in someone's hand)
- Reduced detail in background elements while maintaining the hand-drawn aesthetic
- Abstract shapes used for composition when they share line quality and color treatment with the main illustration
- Environmental suggestions (a simple plant, a window frame) that feel drawn, not designed

**Integration Test:** Before including any element, ask: "Does this look like the same artist drew it?" If an element feels imported from a different visual language (UI design, infographics, stock icons), it does not belong.

### Texture & Pattern

**When to Use:**
- Add warmth and tactility to large flat areas
- Distinguish materials (fabric vs. paper vs. food)
- Create depth without color changes
- Cultural patterns for authenticity (textiles, ceramics)
**How to Execute:**
- Subtle grain/noise textures (10-15% opacity)
- Organic patterns (dots, dashes, cross-hatching) not geometric grids
- Directional texture follows form (shading indicates volume)
- Never texture for decoration alone - must serve clarity or emotion
**Avoid:**
- Heavy textures that reduce legibility
- Photographic/gradient textures
- Busy patterns that compete with main subject
- Texture as default fill (use intentionally)

## Subject Library

### People

**Representation Principles:**
- Diverse ages, body types, skin tones, abilities, cultural backgrounds
- No tokenization - characters should feel like individuals, not demographic checkboxes
- Authentic cultural details (clothing, hair, accessories, settings)
- Range of abilities shown naturally, not as inspirational narratives
**Body Language & Expression:**
- Active, engaged postures (preparing food, using phone, connecting with others)
- Neutral to gently positive expressions (avoid extreme emotions)
- Eye contact with other characters or camera/viewer for connection
- Hands shown doing meaningful activities (not just posed)
- Seated and standing poses both valued equally (accessibility)
**Styling:**
- Simplified facial features (minimal detail for universality)
- Hair and clothing with cultural specificity where relevant
- Avoid overly stylized proportions (realistic human scale)
- Skin tones using coral/stone/orange palette ranges authentically
- Age indicators through posture/styling, not caricature
**Family & Community Context:**
- Multi-generational groupings
- Caregiver relationships shown with dignity
- Shared meals and communal food preparation
- Technology use in family settings (not isolated individuals)

### Food

**Cultural Diversity:**
- Latin American cuisines: beans, rice, tortillas, platanos, mole, tamales
- Soul food traditions: greens, cornbread, sweet potatoes, okra
- Asian cuisines: rice, noodles, dumplings, stir-fries, curries
- Mediterranean foods: olive oil, whole grains, legumes, vegetables
- Avoid “clean eating” stereotypes (quinoa bowls, acai, etc.)
**Visual Treatment:**
- Recognizable, appetizing, realistic proportions
- Warm color palette (coral, orange, terracotta ranges)
- Show food in context (plates, tables, hands preparing)
- Both raw ingredients and prepared meals
- Home cooking environments, not restaurant presentation
**Avoid:**
- Perfect plating or food styling
- “Superfoods” or wellness trend foods as defaults
- Portion size judgment (no “healthy” vs “unhealthy” sizing)
- Isolated food items without human context
- Green smoothies, salads, and stereotypical “diet” foods unless contextually relevant

### Technology & Platform

**Devices:**
- Smartphones (primary) - accessible entry point, voice-first
- Tablets in clinical/community settings
- Laptops for care coordination
- Avoid: wearables, smartwatches (accessibility barriers)
**Interface Elements:**
- Simplified screen representations (no specific UI details)
- Voice interaction indicators (sound waves, microphone icons)
- Notification/connection symbols
- Platform branding subtle, not prominent
**Human-Technology Relationship:**
- Technology as tool in hands, not dominant focus
- Eye contact with people, not screens, when possible
- Devices shown in natural home/community environments
- Multi-generational technology use (not age-segregated)

### Healthcare Elements

**Clinical Credibility:**
- Medical symbols simplified and accessible (not intimidating)
- Care team members shown in approachable contexts
- Clinical settings feel human-scaled, not institutional
- Charts/data visualized simply, never overwhelming
**Avoid:**
- Stethoscopes, lab coats (overly clinical)
- Hospital beds, IV drips (anxiety-inducing)
- Complex medical equipment (intimidating)
- White, sterile environments
**Show Instead:**
- Community health centers
- Home health visits
- Telehealth on family devices
- Simple health tracking (not medicalized)

### Abstract Concepts

**Connection & Support:**
- Linked nodes/circles (network, community)
- Overlapping shapes (collaboration, shared resources)
- Arrows showing flow/progress (not hierarchical)
- Hands offering/receiving (partnership, not charity)
**Progress & Outcomes:**
- Gentle upward movement (growth, improvement)
- Path/journey metaphors (achievable steps)
- Before/during/after as process, not transformation
- Incremental change, not dramatic shifts
**Health & Nutrition:**
- Organic growth forms (plants, leaves)
- Cycles and seasons (sustainable patterns)
- Balance without perfection
- Nourishment as ongoing, not endpoint

## Composition Patterns

### Simple Illustrations (Icons & Spots)

**Purpose:** Decorative accents, UI elements, concept markers, quick communication
**Layout Systems:**
- **Centered/Symmetrical** - Stability, trust (medical concepts, platform features)
- **Off-center/Balanced** - Approachable, human (food, people, activities)
- **Diagonal Energy** - Progress, action (outcomes, growth, connection)
**Negative Space Rules:**
- Minimum 30% breathing room around primary subject
- Use background color intentionally (not just empty)
- Negative space shapes should be as considered as positive
- Test legibility at 64px × 64px minimum
**Focal Points:**
- Single clear focal point (one thing to look at first)
- Supporting elements maximum 40% visual weight of primary
- Use size, color contrast, and detail density to establish hierarchy
- Avoid competing elements of equal visual weight
**Sizing Specifications:**
- Icon minimum: 64px × 64px
- Spot illustration: 200px - 400px wide
- Exportable up to 800px without quality loss
- Maintain 1:1 or 4:3 aspect ratios for consistency

### Complex Narrative Scenes

**Purpose:** Patient journey visualization, care scenario explanation, process illustration, emotional storytelling
**Layout Systems:**
- **Rule of Thirds** - Place key elements at intersections for dynamic balance
- **Z-Pattern** - Guide eye through narrative flow (left-to-right reading culture)
- **Layered Depth** - Foreground/midground/background for environmental context
**Foreground (40% of composition):**
- Primary characters and key actions
- Highest detail density
- Warmest colors (terracotta, coral, orange for people/food)
- Sharpest focus and contrast
**Midground (30% of composition):**
- Supporting characters or contextual elements
- Moderate detail (recognizable but not competing)
- Transitional colors bridging foreground/background
- Secondary narrative moments
**Background (30% of composition):**
- Environmental context and setting
- Minimal detail (suggestions, not specifics)
- Cooler or lighter tones (stone, light teal, soft blue)
- Establishes location without distraction
**Narrative Flow:**
- Left-to-right progression for time/process
- Eye path through color contrast and detail density
- Use overlapping elements to create connections
- Entry point (where eye lands first) in upper left
- Resolution/outcome in lower right
**Scale Relationships:**
- People at realistic proportions to environment (not dwarfed by systems)
- Technology appropriately sized (accessible, not imposing)
- Food and objects at recognizable scale
- Buildings/institutions suggested, not dominating
**Emotional Pacing:**
- Calm areas balance active areas (visual rhythm)
- Use negative space to prevent overwhelm
- Color temperature shifts guide emotional tone
- Avoid edge-to-edge density (breathing room at borders)

## Application-Specific Guidelines

### Investor Pitch Decks

**Function:** Build credibility, communicate outcomes, visualize scale
**Style Requirements:**
- Clean, professional execution (no rough/sketchy quality)
- Data visualization integrated with illustration
- Complex scenes showing care coordination workflows
- Icons for outcome metrics (hospital visits down, costs reduced)
**Color Palette:**
- Lead with Navy and Teal for clinical credibility
- Terracotta for human elements, warmth
- Stone for backgrounds, neutrality
- Limit to 4 colors per slide
**Composition:**
- Horizontal orientation (16:9 slide format)
- Clear visual hierarchy (what to look at first)
- Support data, don't compete with it
- Scalable from presentation screen to printed handout
**Subjects to Emphasize:**
- Care coordination (people + technology + healthcare systems)
- Patient outcomes (progress narratives, not before/after)
- Platform infrastructure (accessible technology)
- Value-based care relationships (partnerships, integration)

**Function:** Build trust, explain processes, reduce anxiety, empower action
**Style Requirements:**
- Maximum warmth and approachability
- Simple, clear communication (no jargon)
- Culturally specific representation
- Bilingual considerations (visual-first, text-second)
**Color Palette:**
- Lead with Sage Green and Terracotta (optimism + warmth)
- Stone backgrounds for calm
- Avoid heavy Navy (too clinical for patient materials)
- Use full coral/orange range for food diversity
**Composition:**
- Vertical orientation for mobile/print handouts
- Generous negative space (not visually overwhelming)

**Subjects to Emphasize:**
- Diverse patients actively engaged in health

**Accessibility:**
- High contrast for low vision

### Platform/Product UI

**Color Palette:**

**Composition:**
- Square or circular bounds for icons (consistent grid)
- Centered, symmetrical layouts for stability
- Minimum detail (recognize at 24px)

**Subjects to Emphasize:**
- Food and meal tracking
- Communication (voice, messages, connection)
- Progress indicators (gentle, non-judgmental)

**Subjects to Emphasize:**
- Care coordination workflows
