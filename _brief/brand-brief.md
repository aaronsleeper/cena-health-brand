# Cena Health — Brand Brief

_Source of truth for all downstream design agents. Derived from logo SVG, content strategy, messaging, voice guidelines, and illustration system seed documents._

---

## 1. Brand Personality

Cena Health is **clinical infrastructure that feels human**. It is not a food delivery company, not a wellness brand, not a tech startup performing empathy. It is a healthcare system layer — plumbing for prescription-based nutrition — that happens to touch people at one of the most intimate points in their lives: what they eat.

**Character traits, in order of priority:**

1. **Credible before charismatic.** Cena Health earns trust through evidence, not enthusiasm. It cites outcomes (30% hospitalization reduction, 25% fewer ER visits) before it tells stories. Its default register is factual.
2. **Warm without softness.** The brand acknowledges real health challenges without flinching, but never reduces patients to their conditions. "Dignified humanity" is not a tagline — it is a visual and verbal constraint.
3. **Culturally specific, not culturally decorative.** The brand names cuisines (mole, platanos, greens, dumplings), not demographics. Cultural competence shows up in the product (disease-specific, culturally relevant care plans) and must show up in every visual and verbal artifact.
4. **Systemic, not heroic.** Cena Health does not save patients. It builds infrastructure that lets providers prescribe nutrition and lets patients engage with care that respects their lives. The brand positions itself as scaffolding, not savior.
5. **Direct.** The voice guidelines are explicit: skip preambles, lead with action, push back when there is a better approach. This is not a brand that hedges.

**What Cena Health is not:**

- Not a D2C wellness brand (no green smoothies, no "clean eating," no aspirational lifestyle imagery)
- Not a generic health tech company (no abstract data visualizations, no "revolutionizing" language)
- Not a charity or social enterprise (it generates revenue through value-based care; equity is a design principle, not a cause)

## 2. Emotional Intent

### First impression
"This is serious healthcare work, but it doesn't feel cold." The viewer should register clinical credibility and human warmth simultaneously — not one after the other. The logo already does this: the organic, concentric petal form paired with a clean wordmark.

### Second impression
"They actually understand the communities they serve." Cultural specificity should become visible quickly — in food imagery, in representation, in language choices. This is where the brand separates from competitors who add "diverse" stock photos as a surface treatment.

### Over time
"I trust this system." The emotional trajectory is from warmth → specificity → reliability. Long-term trust comes from consistency: the brand should feel the same in an investor deck, a patient-facing flyer, and a clinical integration document. Not identical in tone, but recognizably the same entity.

## 3. Audience

### Audience 1: Hospital Systems and Health Plans (Primary B2B)

**Functional needs:** Clinical evidence, ROI data, EHR/FHIR integration capability, compliance documentation, workflow integration for clinical staff.

**Emotional needs:** Confidence that this is proven infrastructure, not a pilot program. Assurance that patient-facing materials will be appropriate for their populations. Fear mitigation — they are buying into an SDOH initiative and need defensible outcomes.

**Brand voice for this audience:** Lead with business fundamentals. Metrics first, narrative second. Reference existing partnerships (UConn Health, Vanderbilt Health) as proof points. Frame everything in terms of utilization reduction and shared savings.

### Audience 2: Investors and Strategic Partners

**Functional needs:** Market size ($17B in preventable costs), competitive differentiation, financial model clarity, team credibility.

**Emotional needs:** Excitement tempered by evidence. They want to see a company that understands it is building infrastructure, not chasing trends. They need to feel the inevitability of the market.

**Brand voice for this audience:** No vision statements without attached metrics. Competitive analysis with concrete differentiators. Professional, evidence-based aesthetic.

### Audience 3: Patients and Families

**Functional needs:** Program clarity, enrollment accessibility, culturally relevant care plans, understandable meal guidance.

**Emotional needs:** Dignity, agency, cultural recognition. They need to feel seen as individuals with specific traditions, not as "diverse populations." They need to feel capable ("you can do this with our help"), not overwhelmed.

**Brand voice for this audience:** Accessible language, no jargon. Visual-first communication. Bilingual considerations. Dignity and agency centered. Technology shown as a tool in their hands, not a system imposed on them.

### Audience 4: Clinical Staff

**Functional needs:** Workflow integration, platform adoption support, clinical documentation, care coordination tools.

**Emotional needs:** Confidence that this will make their work easier, not harder. Trust that the platform is clinically sound.

**Brand voice for this audience:** Start with user scenarios and workflow requirements. Balance detail with readability. State compliance requirements clearly.

### Cross-audience tension

The brand must be simultaneously credible enough for a hospital CFO and warm enough for a grandmother navigating a diet-related chronic condition. This is the central design challenge. The solution is not to split the difference — it is to be fully credible AND fully warm, which requires precision in execution rather than compromise in positioning.

## 5. Design Principles

These govern every visual decision across the system. They are specific to Cena Health and derive from observed tensions in the brand's position.

### Principle 1: Clinical Warmth
Every visual must carry both medical credibility and human approachability. If a design feels purely clinical, add organic form and warmth. If it feels purely friendly, add structure and precision. Neither register alone is acceptable.

### Principle 2: Specificity Over Generality
Show the actual thing, not the category. A specific dish (mole, greens with cornbread) instead of "healthy food." A specific interaction (a grandmother preparing a meal with a grandchild while a phone shows the VozCare platform) instead of "patient engagement." The brand earns trust through particularity.

### Principle 3: Dignified Agency
Every depiction of a person must show them as an active participant, not a passive recipient. This applies to patients, providers, and caregivers. Forward-leaning body language. Hands doing things. Technology held, not looming. The patient is the subject of the sentence, never the object.

### Principle 4: Grounded Optimism
Acknowledge the difficulty of managing chronic conditions without catastrophizing or minimizing. Show mid-journey moments, not before/after transformations. Show effort alongside support. The visual metaphor is a coach walking alongside — never a rescuer, never a cheerleader.

### Principle 5: Organic Precision
The visual language uses organic forms (variable line weight, rounded joins, hand-drawn quality, irregular edges) executed with intentional control. This is not loose or sketchy — it is warm and deliberate. The logo establishes this: organic petal shapes rendered with geometric consistency.

### Principle 6: Infrastructure Humility
The brand visualizes systems as background scaffolding that supports human action, never as the hero. Technology is shown at human scale. Platforms are shown in context (a phone in a kitchen, a tablet in a community health center). Data is in service of narrative, not the reverse.

## 6. Open Questions

These are unresolved tensions or gaps that downstream agents must address with explicit rationale.

### Q1: How green is this brand?
The logo uses four values that range from near-black green through teal to sage. This creates a palette that could read as "environmental/sustainability" if not carefully managed. Cena Health is a healthcare company, not an environmental one. **The Color Theorist must decide** how to expand the palette in ways that anchor the brand in healthcare without losing the organic warmth the greens provide. The teal-to-sage gradient in the logo is the strongest visual signal the brand has — the palette must build from it without becoming monochromatic.

### Q2: Where does the "Cena" / "health" color split land typographically?
The logo uses dark near-black for "Cena" and medium teal for "health." This creates a two-tone wordmark with clear hierarchy: the proper noun is anchored, the descriptor floats in the brand color. **The Typographer must decide** whether this two-tone logic extends into the type system (e.g., headings in dark, body in a different weight/color) or stays isolated to the logo.

### Q3: What is the role of photography?
The illustration system is extensively documented, but photography is never mentioned. For investor materials and clinical documentation, illustration alone may feel insufficient. **Downstream agents must decide** whether photography is part of the system, and if so, what its relationship to the illustration language looks like.

### Q4: How does the brand handle the B2B/B2C visual split?
Hospital system buyers and patients have fundamentally different visual expectations. The illustration system provides tone guidance per context (clean/professional for investors vs. maximum warmth for patients), but does not resolve whether these are variations within one visual system or effectively two different presentations. **The system must maintain recognizable unity** across contexts while allowing appropriate tonal shifts.

### Q5: What happens at the intersection of "hand-drawn organic" and "healthcare infrastructure"?
The illustration principles emphasize hand-drawn quality, variable line weight, and organic imperfection. The brand positioning emphasizes clinical evidence, ROI, and healthcare infrastructure. These are not contradictory, but the execution must be precise: too organic and it feels like a wellness startup, too structured and it loses the warmth that differentiates it. **Every visual execution must be tested against both registers.**

### Q6: How literal is the "no text in illustrations" rule?
The illustration system explicitly prohibits text overlays, standalone icons, and UI-style graphics within illustrations. In practice, showing the VozCare platform or AVA voice assistant in context will require depicting screens with content on them. **The system needs a clear rule** for how UI elements appear within illustrated scenes without violating the thematic cohesion principle.
